Project Overview

BARTHx Corporation represents a groundbreaking GreenTech initiative developed at EMINES - School of Industrial Management within the prestigious MSN Laboratories of UM6P. This project addresses one of the most pressing environmental challenges of our time: efficient CO₂ capture at the source.

Innovation Core: Development of a next-generation chemical reducer leveraging advanced adsorption technology to efficiently capture CO₂ and contribute to the ecological transition.

Unlike traditional carbon capture methods, BARTHx Corporation offers a modular, energy-efficient solution that can be integrated directly into industrial emission sources, transportation systems, and energy production facilities. The technology focuses on direct source capture, making it particularly effective in high-emission environments where workers are exposed to dangerous CO₂ concentrations.

This project combines chemical engineering innovation with industrial management principles to create a viable, scalable solution that addresses both environmental concerns and workplace safety issues in high-emission sectors.

Targeted Sectors & Applications

BARTHx Corporation's technology is designed to address CO₂ emissions in the most critical sectors identified in our comprehensive research:

Industrial Sector

Cement production, steel manufacturing, chemical plants, and refineries where combustion processes and chemical reactions generate massive CO₂ emissions. Workers in confined spaces face significant health risks.

Transportation Sector

Road transport, maritime shipping, and aviation where fossil fuel dependence creates concentrated emissions in urban areas, tunnels, ports, and airports, exposing workers to exhaust gases.

Energy Production

Thermal power plants (coal, gas, oil) that remain major CO₂ sources globally. Despite renewable energy growth, many regions still depend on these emissions-intensive facilities.

Extractive Industries

Mining, oil and gas operations where extraction, transportation, and processing activities generate both direct and indirect emissions in confined or semi-confined environments.

Prototype Development

Our research at EMINES and MSN Laboratories has led to the development of an industrial application prototype, initially focused on automotive integration:

1

Gas Reception & SCR Transformation

Combustion gases containing NOx are transformed into nitrogen, water vapor, and CO₂ through Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) with AdBlue injection.

2

Fine Particle Filtration

Remaining fine particles are captured by an integrated ceramic substrate within the particulate filter, ensuring efficient retention even at high temperatures.

3

CO₂ Capture via BARTHx System

CO₂ produced during SCR reaction is directed to cylindrical pores where it is retained by the X-solution integrated into the substrate design.

4

Thermal Activation & Storage

The captured CO₂ section is heated via rotary fan movement, inducing controlled desorption for recovery and intermediate storage in a stable compartment.

Introduction

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) occupies a central position in debates related to climate change, energy transition, and the sustainability of economic activities. Primarily resulting from fossil fuel combustion, it is massively emitted by key sectors such as industry, transport, energy production, and certain extractive activities.

Beyond its global environmental impact, CO₂ emissions also pose a direct health and safety problem, particularly for workers in high-emission environments. Heavy industrial sectors (cement plants, steel mills, refineries, chemical plants), intensive road and rail transport, tunnels, port areas, and certain confined spaces daily expose employees to high concentrations of gas.

High-Emission Sectors

2.1 Industrial Sector

The industrial sector is among the largest global emitters of CO₂. Heavy industries such as cement, steel, glass, chemical products, and petroleum refining generate significant emissions related to the combustion of coal, natural gas, or fuel oil, as well as chemical reactions intrinsic to production processes.

2.2 Transport Sector

Transport is one of the main contributors to CO₂ emissions, notably road, air, and maritime transport. Dependence on fossil fuels (gasoline, diesel, kerosene, heavy fuel oil) explains the high carbon intensity of this sector.

2.3 Energy Production Sector

Electricity and heat production from fossil fuels remains a major source of CO₂. Coal, gas, and oil thermal power plants release significant quantities of CO₂ into the atmosphere.

2.4 Extractive and Resource Transformation Sectors

Mining, oil, and gas activities also contribute significantly to CO₂ emissions. The extraction, transport, and transformation of fossil resources require energy-intensive equipment and generate direct and indirect emissions.

Current Solutions: Limits and Impacts

Faced with the continuous increase in CO₂ emissions in industrial and transport sectors, several solutions have been implemented in recent decades. These approaches aim either to reduce emissions at source or to limit their dispersion in the atmosphere.

Solution Sector Estimated Impact Main Limits
Filters & Smoke Depollution Industry Reduction of particles > 90% Low efficiency on CO₂
Carbon Capture & Storage (CCS) Industry/Energy Capture up to 95% of CO₂ High costs, high energy consumption, risky storage
Energy Efficiency Improvement Industry/Transport Reduction of 10-30% of emissions Limited gains, ceiling effect
Electric Vehicles Transport Almost total local emission reduction Dependence on electricity mix, high costs
Biofuels Transport Reduction of 20-60% of CO₂ Competition with food, land use
Carbon Compensation All sectors Theoretical emission neutralization Indirect effect, uncertain results
